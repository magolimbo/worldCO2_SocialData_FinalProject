Our contribution to global warming

Burmeister & Wain’s shipyard in Copenhagen, Denmark

Temperatures on Earth have been rising since the Industrial Revolution (1760-1840). While natural variability plays some part, the preponderance of evidence indicates that human activities—particularly emissions of heat-trapping greenhouse gases—are mostly responsible for making our planet warmer. According to NASA - GISS which is an ongoing study on temperature analysis, the average global temperature on Earth has increased by at least 1.1° Celsius (1.9° Fahrenheit) since 1880. The majority of the warming has occurred since 1975, at a rate of roughly 0.15 to 0.20°C per decade. Within this context, our analysis focuses on quantifying the specific contribution of each country of greenhouse gas emissions to this observed temperature increase. By examining the relationship between greenhouse gas emissions and temperature changes over the past century, we aim to explain the extent to which human activities have influenced the Earth’s climate system.

In the beginning of the 20th century, not surprisingly, United States was the leading country in terms of rising the global temperature. The US contributed with 0.04°C, which, indeed is a big part of the contribution as we saw before that the planet is getting warmer by 0.15 to 0.20°C per decade. This contribution was followed by United Kingdom, Russia and Germany, each of them contributing a 0.01°C per the year 1901.

How did the US contribute x4 times more than countries such as UK, Germany or Russia? Let’s dive into history…

By 1878 the United States had reentered a period of prosperity after the long depression of the mid-1870s. In the ensuing 20 years the volume of industrial production, the number of workers employed in industry, and the number of manufacturing plants all more than doubled. The expansion of the iron and steel industry was even more impressive: from 1880 to 1900 the annual production of steel in the United States went from about 1,400,000 to more than 11,000,000 tons. Before the end of the century, the United States surpassed Great Britain.

in the production of iron and steel and was providing more than one-quarter of the world’s supply of pig iron.

In addition, it is interesting to note that in 1996 Russia was overtaken by China, now the second largest contributor in terms of temperature. Why did China overtake Russia? Let’s take a look:

Jiang Zemin and Boris Yeltsin in 1996

In 1996, China surpassed Russia in terms of greenhouse gas emissions, consequently impacting global temperatures. This shift was propelled by China’s rapid industrial and economic growth during the 1990s, driven by expansion in manufacturing, construction, and energy sectors. Meanwhile, Russia’s economic transition following the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 may have temporarily reduced industrial production and emissions. Additionally, Russia’s economic and political challenges during this period could have hindered its capacity to monitor and regulate emissions effectively. Global shifts in energy and environmental policies, such as the impending Kyoto Protocol of 1997, which imposed emission reduction targets on developed nations, may have influenced China and Russia’s emission behaviors.

In conclusion, currently in the year 2024, different countries and continents will adopt measures to curb the creation of greenhouse gases such as the Ban on heavy fuel oil in the Arctic as of July 1, 2024, which states that ships may not use or transport heavy fuel oil as fuel in Arctic waters. But certainly, there is still a long way to go and it will be long, unfortunately, not all countries have the necessary measures in place to fight climate change. World organizations must fight together to stop the climate catastrophe and ban those who do not follow the stipulated regulations.
